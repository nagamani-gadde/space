The Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO) is India's national space agency, founded on August 15, 1969, with its headquarters in  Bengaluru. It replaced the Indian National Committee for Space Research (INCOSPAR), which had been established in 1962 under the  Department of Atomic Energy at the suggestion of Dr. Vikram Sarabhai, widely regarded as the father of the Indian space program.  Early History and Founding Satellite research in India began in the early 1960s when global satellite applications were in their infancy.

              Dr. Vikram Sarabhai led the effort to harness space technology for addressing real-life challenges in India, primarily communication,  broadcasting, meteorology, and resource management. INCOSPAR's first notable accomplishment was the launch of a sounding rocket  from the Thumba Equatorial Rocket Launching Station (TERLS) in 1963. was set up in 1969 to expand the vision and activities  established under INCOSPAR, and later brought under the newly formed Department of Space in 1972.

              Major Milestones ISRO launched its first satellite, Aryabhata, with the help of the Soviet Union on April 19, 1975. In 1980, ISRO successfully launched the Rohini satellite (RS-1) using an indigenous launch vehicle (SLV-3), making India the seventh nation to achieve this feat.In 1980, ISRO successfully launched the Rohini satellite (RS-1) using an indigenous launch vehicle (SLV-3), making India the seventh nation to achieve this feat.

              The space agency developed and expanded its capabilities with a range of rockets: the Satellite Launch Vehicle (SLV), the Polar Satellite Launch Vehicle (PSLV), and the Geostationary Satellite Launch Vehicle (GSLV), as well as their variants for different orbits and payloads.Key Programs and Achievements The Indian National Satellite System (INSAT), launched in 1988, revolutionized India's telecommunications and broadcasting sectors, alongside the Indian Remote Sensing (IRS) program for resource monitoring.Landmark missions include Chandrayaan-1 (2008), India's first lunar orbiter; Mangalyaan or Mars Orbiter Mission (2013), which made India the first Asian nation to reach Martian orbit; Chandrayaan-2 (2019), and Chandrayaan-3 (2023). In January 2025, ISRO successfully performed its first space docking experiment, making India the fourth country to demonstrate this technology.

              Structure and Contributions ISRO operates through a network of research, development, and mission centers, with the main ones being Vikram Sarabhai Space Centre (VSSC), U R Rao Satellite Centre (URSC), Satish Dhawan Space Centre (SDSC), and others across India.Its achievements span satellite design, launch vehicles, remote sensing, navigation (IRNSS/NavIC), meteorology, and international collaborations. ISRO is also preparing for its first manned mission, Gaganyaan, targeted for launch in 2026.

Dr. V. Narayanan

              Dr. V. Narayanan is the 11th Chairman of the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO). He assumed the role on January 13, 2025, succeeding S. Somanath. Dr. Narayanan is a distinguished rocket and spacecraft propulsion expert with a four-decade career at ISRO

              Dr. V. Narayanan, Distinguished Scientist (Apex Grade), assumed the charge of Secretary, Department of Space, Chairman, Space Commission and Chairman, ISRO on the afternoon of 13th January, 2025. Prior to this, he served as Director, Liquid Propulsion Systems Centre (LPSC), one of the major Centres of the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO), with its headquarters at Valiamala in Thiruvananthapuram and a unit at Bangalore. As Director, he provided techno-managerial leadership to LPSC, which is engaged in the development of Liquid, Semi-Cryogenic and Cryogenic Propulsion Stages for Launch Vehicles, Chemical and Electric Propulsion Systems for Satellites, Control Systems for Launch Vehicles and Transducer development for propulsion system health monitoring.
